"Built Around Cambria"

The guildhall smelled of old paper, the kind of smell that came from a building that has been actively used by the local community. Aldric von Reichel had walked the market street before coming inside - four shopfronts without any cameras trailing him. A shoemaker had shown him a boot he was proud of. A grocer had complained about delivery contracts that always seemed to favor whoever already had the most trucks. By the time he reached the guildhall, he had heard the shape of the argument he was about to have before anyone in the room had said a word.
The local council of shop owners sat around a long table that had clearly hosted disputes longer and pettier than this one. Twelve chairs, most of them full. At the head sat Governess Wren, a woman who ran the town's only hardware supplier and, by unspoken consensus, ran most other things too.
"Mr. von Reichel," she said, not rising. "You understand we've heard the word 'union' spoken with great affection by your party before. Some of us in this room employ three people. Some of us employ one - ourselves. We are not sure your affection extends to us."
A murmur of agreement went around the table. Aldric remained standing, hands loose at his sides, no notes.
"It doesn't," he said. "Not in the way you mean it. I am not here to organize your one employee against you, Governess. I am here because I think the thing crushing your margins and the thing crushing a factory floor worker's wages in Cambria is, more often than not, the same thing wearing two different coats."
A man near the far end, younger with a hardware apron still on, came forward.
"Go on, then," he said. "Name the reason."
"Consolidation," Aldric said. "Three delivery firms in this region ten years ago. One now. You all know why your contracts stopped being negotiations and turned into their own terms handed down from a corporation building in Montiablo you've never seen. That is monopoly, not to be confused with a fair free market, and monopoly does not care whether the hand it closes belongs to a shop owner or a shift worker. It closes on both."
Governess Wren still not fully satisfied proceeded to ask
"So what do you propose," she asked. "Beyond a cause we already knew."
"Anti-monopoly enforcement with teeth. Contract protections so a supplier can't be strong-armed by a single buyer with regional leverage. And investment that comes to Cambria instead of past it."
An older woman two seats down from Governess Wren, current owner of the town's textile cooperative, set down her pen.
"Enforcement with teeth," she said. "We've heard that phrase from three different administrations. What does it actually mean? A commission that meets twice a year and writes a report nobody reads? Or something that would have stopped what happened to the delivery firms before it happened?"
Aldric turned to face her directly.
"Before," he said. "a merger or buyout above a set threshold in any sector serving a rural region triggers automatic review, which is different from a simple request. Requests can be quietly avoided by filing under a different registry. Additionally, the review board isn't seated entirely in Montiablo. Regional representatives sit on it. People who'd actually notice three delivery firms becoming one, before the paperwork was finalized instead of after, when it is late to fix it."
The textile cooperative woman considered this, she was no longer dismissive.
"And if the board finds against a merger," she said, "what stops the buyer from simply waiting a season and trying again with better lawyers?"
"Nothing, fully," Aldric admitted. "I won't stand here and tell you a law makes a determined monopolist disappear. But every delay is a season your suppliers keep leverage, you keep alternatives, keep the ability to organize a cooperative buying terms of their own instead of being cornered into one buyer's terms. This law would establish the door into monopolies slower. And a slower door is time you don't currently have."
A heavyset man who had said nothing so far, seated near Wren's right hand finally spoke.
"Contract protections," he said. "Say it plainly. Right now if the one buyer in the region decides my terms next season, I sign or I lose the account entirely, there's no third option. What changes under you?"
"A floor," Aldric said. "Minimum contract terms set by sector, renegotiated with regional input rather than dictated by whoever has the largest share of the route in question. A buyer can still choose not to work with you. They cannot use the absence of alternatives as leverage to set terms below that floor. It removes the worst version of the threat, the negotiation held at your throat."
The man grunted, not quite agreement, but not exactly dismissing.
"Investment," the man in the apron repeated "Everyone promises investment. Then it's a rail line that runs through us on the way to somewhere else."
Aldric nodded slowly.
"That's fair, and it's the right thing to be suspicious of," he said. "So let me be specific instead of comforting. The high-speed line connects Caprica's regions to each other- but Cambria gets its own node, not a platform on someone else's route. Your town stays as the destination."
He paused, then added the second half, the part he'd been saving.
"And alongside it, a research center. Here. In Cambria, not a simple lecture hall built by Montiablo and staffed by people who know nothing about you and your living. Cambrian scientists, working on what Cambria actually needs, your soil, your supply routes, your workshops, with the freedom to set that agenda themselves. An institution that answers to this town and its people before it answers to anyone else."
The cobbler he'd spoken to earlier that morning, invited to sit in, spoke up from a chair near the back.
"Staffed by Cambrians," he said. "How? You can't conjure scientists out of a market street. Half the young people here leave for Montiablo the day they're old enough to and don't come back."
Aldric thought for a second and responded.
"That's exactly why the order matters," he said. "Funded positions here, reserved for Cambrian applicants first, with the university stipends to make it possible for someone from this town to train and come home to something worth coming home to. Right now the incentive runs one direction, leave, and everything worth having is in Montiablo. This reverses that, even slightly. It gives your children a reason to fill out the application from here instead of in a bigger city."
He continued, before anyone could ask the obvious follow-up.
"And on the rail node, since I promised specifics," he said. "It's not a platform someone builds and forgets. Freight capacity for the feed exchange and the textile cooperative both, timed to your harvest and production cycles rather than a fixed citywide schedule designed around the convenience of others. A stop that exists because Cambria has cargo worth stopping for, or so I believe your cargo is worth stopping for."
The feed and grain man's eyebrows rose slightly at that.
"Timed to harvest," he repeated. "Not the others’ schedule stapled onto us."
"Not stapled onto you," Aldric confirmed. "Built around you."
Governess Wren spoke again.
"You understand," she said, "that we have been promised things before, and the people who promised them stopped visiting once the ballots were counted."
"I know," Aldric said. "I'm not asking you to trust the promise. I'm asking you to hold me to it, in this room, next year, and every year after. That's a better offer than trust. It's accountability. And I promise to visit rural town halls every year, so you may hold me accountable and tell your complaints to my face, instead of sending me mails, which I do not really check."
When the meeting broke and the shop owners filed out into the thinning rain, the young man in the apron lingered a moment at the door.
"The research center," he said. "The people running it, actually from here?"
"Actually from here," Aldric confirmed.
The man considered this, nodded once.

"The Line Runs Both Ways"

The field stretched flat and gold and Aldric von Reichel had left the car at the road rather than have it grind down the access track. He walked the last stretch on foot, by the time he reached the workers stringing wire along a fence line, he had already decided not to open with a speech.
There was no AVIS or any other news agency crew trailing him or any bodyguards. A rural district didn't want to watch itself being performed to, it wanted to see whether a man would still show up when there was no audience to perform for. The road out here had been unpaved for the last two miles, and the dust on his coat was doing more persuasive work than anything he was about to say.
Two men and a woman were working the fence, the kind of task that took a whole morning. The older of the two men, tired, noticed him first and straightened..
"You're the one from the papers," he said. "The SDA man. Didn't expect you'd get your boots dirty for it."
"Seemed dishonest to talk about Cambria from a stage," Aldric said, "when the actual work is out here."
The woman, younger, wiped her hands on her trousers and studied him for a moment before.
"We heard about the rail line," she said. "Everyone's heard about the rail line. What we haven't heard is whether it stops here or just goes past us on the way to somewhere that matters more."
"It stops here," Aldric said. "I can walk you to the exact site, if you've got twenty minutes and don't mind the company."
The older man snorted and set down his wire cutters.
"Fence isn't going anywhere," he said. "Go on, then. Show us."
The proposed node sat a fifteen-minute walk further out, a stretch of open ground marked at its corners with survey stakes and faded orange tape. Aldric stopped at the center of it and turned in a slow half circle.
"Here," he said. "Not a platform on the way to one of those big cities. A stop built because Cambria has cargo worth stopping for, your grain, your textiles, your goods, and passengers worth carrying both directions instead of just outgoing."
"Both directions," the woman repeated. "Meaning what, exactly? A train that stops so someone from the city can look at us on their way through?"
"Meaning freight capacity timed to your harvest and production cycles" Aldric said. "And meaning it's cheaper and faster for someone in Cambria to reach opportunity in other places without having to become a resident elsewhere to access it. The connection runs both ways, or it isn't worth building."
The older man crouched, picked up a handful of dry soil, and let it run through his fingers, thinking.
"And the town itself," he said. "Because a node like this, I've seen what it does to a place. Suddenly everyone wants to build near it, prices climb, and five years later the town isn't the town anymore. It's a suburb with a station."
Aldric took a moment before answering, giving it weight.
"That's the actual risk," he admitted. "And I won't pretend a rail stop is neutral, it changes a place, always. What I can commit to is that the node connects Cambria to the network without making Cambria's governance answerable to it. No absorption into a larger administrative district built around the station. Zoning and development around the node stay under Cambria's own authority, not handed to a regional planning board seated in the capital. You get the connection. You don't lose the town to it."
The woman folded her arms, weighing it.
"Promises about zoning are easy to make standing in an empty field," she said, not unkindly. "Harder to keep once the surveyors and the money show up."
"Harder," Aldric agreed, "which is exactly why it has to be written into the charter before the first shovel goes into the ground, not negotiated after there's money on the table.
By midday he'd reached the covered well at the center of the smaller settlement past the field, the closest thing the area had to a gathering point, a stone trough, a bench worn smooth, and enough shade to draw four or five people at any given hour. By the time he arrived, a small, loose group had already gathered without the formality of the guildhall just a couple days before.
An old woman on the bench, resting a basket of greens beside her, looked him over with open curiosity rather than suspicion.
"You're the one talking about the research building," she said. "My granddaughter mentioned it. Said something about scientists studying our own soil."
"That's the idea," Aldric said, sitting on the low wall across from her. "A regional center, staffed first by people from here, working on what actually matters to Cambria, your soil, your crops, the problems specific to this ground, not whatever other labs find interesting that year."
A younger man leaning against the well wall spoke up next.
"And if the soil research finds something the big agricultural firms don't like," he asked. "Who does the center actually answer to, when it comes down to it? Because everything eventually answers to somebody."
It was a fair question, and a harder one than it sounded.
"To a regional board with Cambrian seats holding the majority," he said. "Not to a ministry, and not to whichever firm has the loudest lobbyist that season. If the research says something inconvenient for a large buyer, the center doesn't answer to that buyer. It's the entire point of building it here instead of importing findings from somewhere else."
The old woman on the bench nodded slowly and picked her basket back up.
"We've had people come through with plans before," she said. "Most of them left with the season. You planning on being one of the ones who comes back?"
"I'm planning on it being written down where you can check," Aldric said, "so it doesn't depend on whether I come back or not. Though, I will be here to hear your suggestions and complaints every year."
"Fair enough," she said, still thinking about what she was promised.
Aldric stayed for a while, long enough that the conversation drifted away from rail and R&D Centers entirely, into the old woman's granddaughter's schooling, the younger man's opinion on a neighboring farm's dispute, small things that had nothing to do with a campaign and everything to do with a small town. Aldric said little during that stretch, mostly listened. He finally rose to walk back toward the road after a while, no one asked him for a slogan on the way out.
That, he thought, walking the dirt track back toward the car was probably the more honest measure of the day’s work.
