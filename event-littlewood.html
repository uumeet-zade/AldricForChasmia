"The Line that Stays Rural"

Aldric von Reichel discusses High-Speed Rail in Littlewood.

The road into Littlewood, one of Chasmia's smaller rural communities, narrows from paved highway to gravel long before the town square comes into view. Aldric von Reichel's car slows to match the road, and by the time he steps out near the old grain depot - which is repurposed as a community hall - a small crowd has already gathered. Farmers in from the morning's work, a schoolteacher who closed her classroom early for the visit, and the town's aging mayor, Bertrand Oyelin, who greets Von Reichel with a firm handshake and, naturally, some skepticism.

"Rail money always goes to the cities first," Bertrand says, before any formal greeting is even finished. "We've heard the promise before. Roads that never get paved. Lines that stop two towns short of here."

Von Reichel leans into the conversation, pointing toward the depot behind them. "That's exactly why I wanted to start the conversation here, not in Lisieux. If the plan doesn't work for Littlewood specifically, it doesn't work at all."

The crowd is unconvinced but listening, which is enough to start. Bertrand leads Von Reichel through the town - past the shuttered second grain silo, past the schoolhouse with only a single working generator, past a produce stand run by a farmer named Sela Kohrt, who has driven two hours that morning just to sell root vegetables at the town's small market, since Littlewood has no rail or reliable freight connection of its own.

Sela doesn't wait to be asked. "You want to know what infrastructure means to us? It means I spend half my week driving instead of farming. My cousin two towns over gave up selling to the city market entirely - not worth the fuel anymore."

Von Reichel picks up one of her vegetables, turning it over the way he did with the leather wallet in Morrick days earlier, and asks her directly what would actually change her situation in her own terms.

"A line that doesn't skip us," she says. "Doesn't have to be fancy. Just has to stop here."

Von Reichel gathers the group loosely around the depot steps, standing at their level, and lays out the High-Speed Rail plan directly against Littlewood's specific complaint:

"Here's the commitment, plainly: this isn't a rail line built for Lisieux that happens to pass near you. It's designed around connecting communities like Littlewood directly into the wider network - not as an afterthought stop, but as a real node. That means Sela's vegetables reach the Chasmia market and beyond in hours, not half a day of driving. It means your children don't have to leave Littlewood behind entirely just to find work - they can commute, they can ship goods out, they can stay rooted here while still reaching the opportunities the cities have."

Bertrand, arms still crossed, asks the obvious follow-up: what stops this from being the same broken promise as the last three governments who talked about rail expansion and never delivered past the drawing board?

Von Reichel ties it back to the same anti-monopoly economic platform from the Morrick market visit days before, making the connection explicit rather than treating rural policy as a separate silo:

"Because this isn't just a transit project sitting alone - it's tied to the same fight I've been having for the small business owners. Big logistics networks already have the infrastructure to move goods fast and cheap; small producers and small towns don't. If we build this rail line, we're not just laying track, we're closing that gap. Rural development and small business protection are the same policy wearing two names - give people like Sela and people like the shopkeepers in town the same reach the big players already have, without needing their permission or their prices."

The schoolteacher, Marisol Devane, speaks up for the first time, asking a quieter question: does "connecting to the cities" mean Littlewood eventually stops being Littlewood - that growth just means becoming a suburb of somewhere bigger, losing what makes the town itself worth staying in.

Von Reichel pauses before answering and is visibly aware this is the actual fear underneath Bertrand's skepticism too.

"No - and that's the part I want to be clearest about. This isn't about turning Littlewood into an extension of the city. It's about giving you the choice to stay small and connected, instead of small and cut off. Right now the only way to grow is to leave. The rail line exists so that staying here doesn't mean being left behind economically. Your community keeps its shape - its size, its character - it just isn't isolated from opportunity anymore."

That distinction visibly lands differently than the earlier thoughts. Bertrand asks a practical, administrative question about proposed stop locations and construction timelines, which Von Reichel takes as an opening to talk specifics rather than platitudes: rough phasing, priority given to underserved rural nodes rather than only high-traffic urban corridors, and coordination with local town councils on stop placement rather than a top-down route imposed without consultation.

As the visit winds toward its close, Von Reichel walks the depot floor once more, this time with Sela and a few other vendors, discussing - informally, without cameras crowding in - what kind of freight capacity a stop in Littlewood would realistically need to serve farmers like her. It's a small, non-fancy, casual conversation, deliberately so.

Before leaving, Von Reichel addresses the gathered reporters from the town, briefly:

"Littlewood doesn't need to become a city to matter to me and Caprica's future. It needs to stop being an afterthought. High-Speed Rail, done right, isn't about erasing towns like this one - it's about making sure staying here isn't an economic punishment like they want it to be like. That's what rural development actually means, and that's the same principle behind every policy I am running on, from Morrick to right here in Littlewood."

As the car pulls away down the gravel road, Bertrand is overheard telling Sela that it was "the first time in years someone came here and let the town lead the conversation"
