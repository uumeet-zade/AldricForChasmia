"The Line Runs Both Ways"

Aldric von Reichel speaks with locals in the fields of Cambria.

The field stretched flat and gold and Aldric von Reichel had left the car at the road rather than have it grind down the access track. He walked the last stretch on foot. By the time he reached the workers stringing wire along a fence line, he had already decided not to open with a speech.

There was no AVIS or any other news agency crew trailing him or any bodyguards. A rural district didn't want to watch itself being performed to, it wanted to see whether a man would still show up when there was no audience to perform for. The road out here had been unpaved for the last two miles, and the dust on his coat was doing more persuasive work than anything he was about to say.

Two men and a woman were working the fence, the kind of task that took a whole morning. The older of the two men, tired, noticed him first and straightened.

"You're the one from the papers," he said. "The SDA man. Didn't expect you'd get your boots dirty for it."

"Seemed dishonest to talk about Cambria from a stage," Aldric said, "when the actual work is out here."

The woman, younger, wiped her hands on her trousers and studied him for a moment.

"We heard about the rail line," she said. "Everyone's heard about the rail line. What we haven't heard is whether it stops here or just goes past us on the way to somewhere that matters more."

"It stops here," Aldric said. "I can walk you to the exact site, if you've got twenty minutes and don't mind the company."

The older man snorted and set down his wire cutters.

"Fence isn't going anywhere," he said. "Go on, then. Show us."

The proposed node sat a fifteen-minute walk further out, a stretch of open ground marked at its corners with survey stakes and faded orange tape. Aldric stopped at the center of it and turned in a slow half circle.

"Here," he said. "Not a platform on the way to one of those big cities. A stop built because Cambria has cargo worth stopping for, your grain, your textiles, your goods, and passengers worth carrying both directions instead of just outgoing."

"Both directions," the woman repeated. "Meaning what, exactly? A train that stops so someone from the city can look at us on their way through?"

"Meaning freight capacity timed to your harvest and production cycles," Aldric said. "And meaning it's cheaper and faster for someone in Cambria to reach opportunity in other places without having to become a resident elsewhere to access it. The connection runs both ways, or it isn't worth building."

The older man crouched, picked up a handful of dry soil, and let it run through his fingers, thinking.

"And the town itself," he said. "Because a node like this, I've seen what it does to a place. Suddenly everyone wants to build near it, prices climb, and five years later the town isn't the town anymore. It's a suburb with a station."

Aldric took a moment before answering, giving it weight.

"That's the actual risk," he admitted. "And I won't pretend a rail stop is neutral, it changes a place, always. What I can commit to is that the node connects Cambria to the network without making Cambria's governance answerable to it. No absorption into a larger administrative district built around the station. Zoning and development around the node stay under Cambria's own authority, not handed to a regional planning board seated in the capital. You get the connection. You don't lose the town to it."

The woman folded her arms, weighing it.

"Promises about zoning are easy to make standing in an empty field," she said, not unkindly. "Harder to keep once the surveyors and the money show up."

"Harder," Aldric agreed, "which is exactly why it has to be written into the charter before the first shovel goes into the ground, not negotiated after there's money on the table."

By midday he'd reached the covered well at the center of the smaller settlement past the field, the closest thing the area had to a gathering point, a stone trough, a bench worn smooth, and enough shade to draw four or five people at any given hour. By the time he arrived, a small, loose group had already gathered without the formality of the guildhall just a couple days before.

An old woman on the bench, resting a basket of greens beside her, looked him over with open curiosity rather than suspicion.

"You're the one talking about the research building," she said. "My granddaughter mentioned it. Said something about scientists studying our own soil."

"That's the idea," Aldric said, sitting on the low wall across from her. "A regional center, staffed first by people from here, working on what actually matters to Cambria, your soil, your crops, the problems specific to this ground, not whatever other labs find interesting that year."

A younger man leaning against the well wall spoke up next.

"And if the soil research finds something the big agricultural firms don't like," he asked. "Who does the center actually answer to, when it comes down to it? Because everything eventually answers to somebody."

It was a fair question, and a harder one than it sounded.

"To a regional board with Cambrian seats holding the majority," he said. "Not to a ministry, and not to whichever firm has the loudest lobbyist that season. If the research says something inconvenient for a large buyer, the center doesn't answer to that buyer. It's the entire point of building it here instead of importing findings from somewhere else."

The old woman on the bench nodded slowly and picked her basket back up.

"We've had people come through with plans before," she said. "Most of them left with the season. You planning on being one of the ones who comes back?"

"I'm planning on it being written down where you can check," Aldric said, "so it doesn't depend on whether I come back or not. Though, I will be here to hear your suggestions and complaints every year."

"Fair enough," she said, still thinking about what she was promised.

Aldric stayed for a while, long enough that the conversation drifted away from rail and R&D Centers entirely, into the old woman's granddaughter's schooling, the younger man's opinion on a neighboring farm's dispute, small things that had nothing to do with a campaign and everything to do with a small town. Aldric said little during that stretch, mostly listened. He finally rose to walk back toward the road after a while. No one asked him for a slogan on the way out.

That, he thought, walking the dirt track back toward the car was probably the more honest measure of the day's work.
